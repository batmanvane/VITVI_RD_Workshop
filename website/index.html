About

Not your typical academic symposium

We are stepping away from polished, high-level presentations to create a space for deep technical discussions, networking, and practical problem-solving. This is a safe space to share what's actually hard about your research.

Problem & Breakthrough Pitches

PhD students present their actual roadblocks or key findings — not polished results. The audience becomes your brain-trust.

Impulse Talks

Short, provocative and radical input from academia, industry, and startups to keep the energy high and the perspectives diverse.

Open Discussions

Unstructured time for 1-on-1 whiteboarding, spontaneous collaborations, and deep-dive conversations.

The Format

Your 30 Minutes

Every PhD gets 30 minutes — 10 to present, 20 for the room to dig in. Pick the track that fits where you are:

  • Problem Pitch You're stuck. Pitch your roadblock and let the room help you break through.
  • Breakthrough Pitch You've broken through. Share your finding and let the room stress-test it.
Problem Pitch
10 min Your Presentation

Strict 4-slide format — pitch your roadblock, not your results.

  1. The Context (2 min)

    What is the overarching goal of your project?

  2. The Core Roadblock (3 min)

    One or two specific technical problems you're facing right now.

  3. What I've Tried (3 min)

    What solutions have you attempted? Why did they fail or fall short?

  4. The Ask (2 min)

    Specific questions for the room's brain-trust — "What am I missing? Has anyone solved something similar? What would you try next?"

20 min Group Troubleshooting

~10 min Diagnosis: The room asks clarifying questions, reframes the problem, and zeroes in on the real blocker.
~10 min Brainstorm: Concrete suggestions, alternative approaches, tools to try, people to talk to.

Breakthrough Pitch
10 min Your Presentation

5-slide format — share a key finding and give the room something to challenge.

  1. The Context (2 min)

    What is the overarching goal of your project?

  2. The Finding (2 min)

    The core insight or result you want to share.

  3. How You Got There (3 min)

    The method, the path, what didn't work first.

  4. Why It Matters (1.5 min)

    Significance, implications, limitations you already see.

  5. The Challenge (1.5 min)

    Prompts for the room: "Where does this break? Where else could this apply?"

20 min Stress-Test & Transfer

~10 min Stress-Test: The room plays devil's advocate — challenge the methodology, question assumptions, poke holes.
~10 min Transfer: Where else could this apply? Other domains, other students' problems, industry use cases.

The goal is not to prove you know everything — the goal is to leverage the brains in the room to help you break through or build on what you've found.

Problem Pitch

"Can I Trust My Own Simulation Data?"

Katja Müller, TH Brandenburg · ASME J. Turbomachinery, 2025

Slide 1 — The Context
I'm investigating how fan blade geometry influences in-flight ice accretion on the NASA R67 low-pressure compressor rotor. The goal is to run a sensitivity study across 50 blade designs (DoE) to identify which geometric parameters drive ice buildup — and feed those insights into a multidisciplinary design optimization.

The simulation chain is fully automated: ParaBlade generates geometry, ANSYS CFX computes the flow field, and FENSAP-ICE handles droplet trajectories and ice growth.
Slide 2 — The Core Roadblock
My problem is trust in my own data.

To run 50 designs in a reasonable timeframe, I need a manageable grid resolution. But my grid independence study revealed alarming discrepancies:
Fine gridCoarse gridMedium grid
Ice mass1.64 g (ref)3.56 g (+117%)1.71 g (+4%)
Comp. time~6 h~10 min~4 h
The medium grid takes 4 hours per design — that's 200 hours for my 50-design DoE, just for the icing step. How wrong can my rankings be before the whole sensitivity study becomes meaningless?
Slide 3 — What I've Tried
  • Used the medium grid (1×106 nodes) as a compromise — but I don't know if the ~4% error is systematic or design-dependent.
  • Compared against published experimental data (Strazisar et al.) — matches at design point but diverges near stall.
  • Checked statistical indicators (Spearman correlations, CoI) — plausible but with only 50 samples, I can't be sure the rankings are stable.
  • Considered running a subset on the fine grid for spot-checking — but no established method for deciding which designs to validate.
Slide 4 — The Ask
  1. How do you handle simulation fidelity vs. budget trade-offs in DoE work? Is there a principled way to quantify "ranking error" from a coarser grid?
  2. Has anyone used adaptive sampling or surrogate models (GPs, etc.) to reduce the number of full-fidelity runs needed?
  3. Industry perspective: What's your threshold for "good enough" data quality in large parameter studies?
  4. Does anyone have experience with multi-fidelity approaches — coarse-grid as cheap predictor, fine-grid only where it matters?
Problem Pitch

"Balancing Breaks My Best Blades"

Clara Henkel, BTU Cottbus-Senftenberg · Henkel et al., GPPS Shanghai 2025

Slide 1 — The Context
Fan-blisk design optimization where two disciplines collide: aerodynamics wants the most efficient blade shape, structural mechanics needs to balance the blisk by shifting blade sections to avoid stress hotspots. The shifts from balancing change the aerodynamics — so sequential optimization doesn't work.

The blade is parameterized with 16 design variables. CFD runs on Rolls-Royce's HYDRA solver (~45 min/design), FE stress analysis via sc03 (~15 min). We need surrogates to make global optimization feasible.
Slide 2 — The Core Roadblock
No single surrogate works for both objectives — and the stress constraint is the real headache.

Bayesian ridge dominates for efficiency (R² = 0.98) but is the worst for stress (R² = 0.58). SVR wins for stress (R² = 0.88) but is mediocre for efficiency. I'm forced to mix different model types in the same optimization loop.

Worse: stress can be framed as classification (feasible/infeasible) or regression — and they give different answers about which designs are safe. With only 300 CFD samples in a 16-dimensional design space, I can't tell which formulation to trust.
Slide 3 — What I've Tried
  • Tested 5 surrogate types (RF, kNN, SVR, MLP, Bayesian ridge) with full hyperparameter search — no single model is universally best, confirming the no-free-lunch theorem in practice.
  • Classification vs. regression for stress: Classification finds safe/unsafe regions well (AUC = 0.94) but loses magnitude information. Regression preserves magnitude but is less accurate near the critical stress threshold.
  • Reduced 2D validation: Surrogates work beautifully here (R² > 0.96). But scaling to the full 16D problem degrades accuracy significantly.
Slide 4 — The Ask
  1. How do you handle mixed-surrogate optimization where different objectives need fundamentally different model types?
  2. Regression vs. classification for constraints — has anyone found a principled way to decide, or a way to combine both?
  3. Industry: When surrogate-based optimization proposes a design near the stress limit, what's your validation protocol before you trust it?
  4. With 300 samples in 16 dimensions, are we data-starved? Has anyone used multi-fidelity surrogates to bootstrap from cheaper simulations?
Breakthrough Pitch

"We Made SHAP Reliable — Even With 25 Samples"

Klaus Markgraf, TH Brandenburg · Markgraf et al., 2026 (preprint)

Slide 1 — The Context
We use data-driven surrogates to replace expensive CFD in aero-engine development — predicting performance across hundreds of blade designs without running every simulation. To understand why the model predicts what it predicts, we use SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations) to rank which geometric features matter most.

The vision: use XAI not just to predict, but to guide the engineer's intuition — which parameters to focus on, which to ignore in the next design iteration.
Slide 2 — The Finding
BIFI (Bootstrap-Informed Feature Importance) makes SHAP rankings stable and actionable, even with tiny datasets.

The method: train 50 surrogate models on bootstrap resamples, compute SHAP for each, then automatically find the threshold between "important" and "negligible" features using the cumulative SHAP distribution — no manual cutoff needed. BIFI-based DoEs improve surrogate quality (R² and MSE) by up to 90%.
Slide 3 — How I Got There
  • Started with standard SHAP: Same dataset, different random seeds — feature x5 jumps from "most important" to "irrelevant." With 25–50 samples, a single SHAP run is a point estimate with unknown variance.
  • Tried bootstrap resampling: Training B=50 models on resamples and computing SHAP for each revealed which features are reliably important vs. noise.
  • Key insight: The cumulative SHAP distribution has a natural "elbow" that separates signal from noise. BIFI automates finding that elbow — no manual cutoff needed.
  • Validated on four synthetic benchmarks of increasing complexity, then on a real aero-engine dataset.
Slide 4 — Why It Matters
  • Removes human guesswork from feature selection for DoE construction and design space reduction.
  • Works model-agnostically — doesn't matter if the surrogate is a Random Forest, GPR, or neural network.
  • Reduces the number of expensive CFD runs needed downstream by focusing DoE on the features that actually matter.
  • Limitation: Only validated on synthetic benchmarks + one real dataset so far.
Slide 5 — The Challenge
  1. Stress-test: The synthetic benchmarks have known ground truth — but in real engineering problems, how do we verify the "right" features were selected?
  2. Transfer: Could BIFI work for your feature selection problems — outside of turbomachinery? Any domain with expensive simulations and scarce data?
  3. Industry: Would you trust an automated feature importance method in a safety-critical design process? What level of explainability do you need?
Breakthrough Pitch

"From 100 Hours to 4 Minutes: Predicting Blade Performance from Control Points"

Hamun R. Bertram, TH Brandenburg · Bertram et al., ASME Turbo Expo 2025

Slide 1 — The Context
Designing turbomachinery blades requires predicting aerodynamic performance (efficiency, pressure ratio, mass flow) for hundreds of candidate geometries. Running CFD for each takes ~33 hours per batch. ML surrogates promise to replace these simulations, but existing approaches — using dense surface meshes or point clouds as input — need 1,000+ simulations and 24–100 hours of training time.
Slide 2 — The Finding
Use B-Spline control points instead of surface meshes as ML input. Training drops from 100+ hours to 4 minutes — with competitive accuracy from just ~150 simulations.

Each blade is defined by 96 control points (vs. 830,000 mesh cells). A Graph Convolutional Neural Network (GCNN) learns directly from this compact representation. Adding first-order (gradients) and second-order (Laplacians) geometric derivatives captures the curvature physics that drives aerodynamic behavior.
Slide 3 — How I Got There
  • Existing approaches are expensive: Gouttiere et al. achieve good accuracy but take ~102 hours to train. Beqiraj et al. need 1,600 simulations and 24 hours on an RTX 8000.
  • Key insight: ParaBlade's B-Spline representation is already a compact, physics-aware description. Why feed the model 830k mesh points when 96 control points carry the same geometric information?
  • Gradients and Laplacians were the missing piece — raw control points alone aren't enough. The derivatives encode curvature, which directly relates to flow separation, shock formation, and boundary layer behavior.
  • Validated on a 500-design DoE of NASA Rotor 67, with Random Forest Regression as a baseline.
Slide 4 — Why It Matters
  • Training on an Nvidia RTX 4000 in 4 minutes (99.9% reduction) means you can retrain as new CFD results come in — even while simulations are still running.
  • Only ~150–200 converged simulations needed for reliable predictions — practical for real design campaigns.
  • The RFR baseline also benefits: R² = 0.82 on CPU-only hardware — no GPU needed.
  • Limitation: Validated only on NASA R67. The rotor's high twist makes it geometrically complex, but other blade types remain untested.
Slide 5 — The Challenge
  1. Stress-test: R² ≈ 0.72 for GCNN on the test set — good enough for screening, but is it good enough for final design decisions? Where does the model break?
  2. Transfer: This works for B-Spline-parameterized blades. Could the same compact-representation idea apply to your geometries — turbine blades, vanes, other parameterized shapes?
  3. Industry: At what accuracy level would you trust an ML surrogate to replace CFD in your design loop?

Your Preparation Packet

Brain Food

No homework — just three provocations to chew on before you arrive. Pick your approach and be ready to defend it.

1. White Box, Black Box, or Fifty Shades of Gray?

Your team needs a model that predicts fan blade performance across hundreds of design variants. The mechanistic model (RANS solver) is trustworthy but takes 45 min per design. A neural network trained on 80 existing runs is fast but occasionally predicts physically impossible results. Your project lead asks: "Where should we invest our next 3 months — better physics, more data, or something in between?"

A) Pure mechanistic — invest in a reduced-order model (POD/ROM), faster but still physics-based
B) Pure data-driven — run 200 more CFD cases and train a bigger neural net. Let the data speak.
C) Gray-box — use the physics model as backbone, let ML learn the residual (corrective source term)
D) PINN — embed the governing PDEs directly into the loss function, train on sparse data

Read up: Physics-based & data-driven hybrid modeling (review) · SMT — Surrogate Modeling Toolbox (Python)

2. Three Scalings, Three Truths

Your design space has features spanning 6 orders of magnitude: pressure in Pa, angles in degrees, thickness in mm. Three team members preprocess differently — min-max, z-score, and physics-based non-dimensionalization (Mach, Reynolds). They run the same model. They get three different feature importance rankings. Which scaling tells the truth — or does "feature importance" depend on how you ask?

A) Always non-dimensionalize first — let physics decide the scale
B) Use permutation importance — it's model-agnostic and scale-invariant
C) Bootstrap the analysis (BIFI-style) — if the ranking flips with different scaling, it was never robust
D) Drop importance entirely — use SHAP interaction values or causal inference instead

Read up: Importance of Feature Scaling — scikit-learn · Permutation Feature Importance — scikit-learn

3. The Latent Space Looks Suspiciously Tidy

You compress 200 blade geometries (168 parameters) through a VAE into 4 latent dimensions. It reveals 3 clean clusters. Your senior engineer nods: "Those are our three design families." But you trained on your existing designs only. Is the VAE discovering real structure — or just memorizing your design habits?

A) Validate externally — generate samples between clusters and run CFD. Do they behave as predicted?
B) Perturbation test — add noise to the inputs. Do the clusters survive or dissolve?
C) Compare to PCA — if linear decomposition finds the same clusters, you don't need a VAE
D) Adversarial check — feed in geometries from a completely different engine family. Where do they land?

Read up: Design Space Exploration with VAEs · SHAP — Interpretable Machine Learning Book
