About

Not your typical academic symposium

We are stepping away from polished, high-level presentations to create a space for deep technical discussions, networking, and practical problem-solving. This is a safe space to share what's actually hard about your research.

Problem Pitches

PhD students present their actual roadblocks — not their results. The audience becomes your brain-trust.

Impulse Talks

Short, provocative and radical input from academia, industry, and startups to keep the energy high and the perspectives diverse.

Open Troubleshooting

Unstructured time for 1-on-1 whiteboarding, spontaneous collaborations, and deep-dive conversations.

The Format

The Anti-Pitch: 30 Minutes Per Student

10 min Your Presentation

Strict 4-slide format — pitch your roadblock, not your results.

  1. The Context (2 min)

    What is the overarching goal of your project? Keep it brief — just enough context for the room to understand your problem.

  2. The Core Roadblock (3 min)

    Zoom in on one or two specific technical problems you're facing right now. Buggy model? Dirty data? Methodological flaw? Hardware limitation?

  3. What I've Tried (3 min)

    What solutions have you attempted so far? Why did they fail or fall short?

  4. The Ask (2 min)

    Specific questions for the room. "Has anyone used X to solve Y?" or "How do you handle this type of noise in industry?"

20 min Group Troubleshooting

The audience becomes your brain-trust. Open floor for questions, suggestions, war stories, and live debugging. PhDs, industry experts, and startup founders all pitch in.

The goal is not to prove you know everything — the goal is to leverage the brains in the room to help you break through your roadblock.

Examples

What an Anti-Pitch Looks Like

Here are three examples of what we're looking for — honest, specific, technically grounded, and focused on real roadblocks rather than polished results.

"Can I Trust My Own Simulation Data?"

Katja Müller, TH Brandenburg · ASME J. Turbomachinery, 2025

Slide 1 — The Context
I'm investigating how fan blade geometry influences in-flight ice accretion on the NASA R67 low-pressure compressor rotor. The goal is to run a sensitivity study across 50 blade designs (DoE) to identify which geometric parameters drive ice buildup — and feed those insights into a multidisciplinary design optimization.

The simulation chain is fully automated: ParaBlade generates geometry, ANSYS CFX computes the flow field, and FENSAP-ICE handles droplet trajectories and ice growth.
Slide 2 — The Core Roadblock
My problem is trust in my own data.

To run 50 designs in a reasonable timeframe, I need a manageable grid resolution. But my grid independence study revealed alarming discrepancies:
Fine gridCoarse gridMedium grid
Ice mass1.64 g (ref)3.56 g (+117%)1.71 g (+4%)
Comp. time~6 h~10 min~4 h
The medium grid takes 4 hours per design — that's 200 hours for my 50-design DoE, just for the icing step. How wrong can my rankings be before the whole sensitivity study becomes meaningless?
Slide 3 — What I've Tried
  • Used the medium grid (1×106 nodes) as a compromise — but I don't know if the ~4% error is systematic or design-dependent.
  • Compared against published experimental data (Strazisar et al.) — matches at design point but diverges near stall.
  • Checked statistical indicators (Spearman correlations, CoI) — plausible but with only 50 samples, I can't be sure the rankings are stable.
  • Considered running a subset on the fine grid for spot-checking — but no established method for deciding which designs to validate.
Slide 4 — The Ask
  1. How do you handle simulation fidelity vs. budget trade-offs in DoE work? Is there a principled way to quantify "ranking error" from a coarser grid?
  2. Has anyone used adaptive sampling or surrogate models (GPs, etc.) to reduce the number of full-fidelity runs needed?
  3. Industry perspective: What's your threshold for "good enough" data quality in large parameter studies?
  4. Does anyone have experience with multi-fidelity approaches — coarse-grid as cheap predictor, fine-grid only where it matters?

"My AI Explains — but Can I Believe It?"

Klaus Markgraf, TH Brandenburg · Markgraf et al., 2026 (preprint)

Slide 1 — The Context
We use data-driven surrogate models to replace expensive CFD simulations in aero-engine development — predicting performance across hundreds of blade designs without running every simulation. To understand why the model makes certain predictions, we use SHAP (Shapley Additive Explanations) to rank which geometric features matter most.

The vision: use XAI to not just predict, but to guide the engineer's intuition — tell us which parameters to focus on, and which to ignore in the next design iteration.
Slide 2 — The Core Roadblock
SHAP values are unstable when data is scarce — and in engine development, data is always scarce.

Running the same SHAP analysis with different random seeds on the same 25-sample dataset gives wildly different feature rankings. Feature x5 jumps from "most important" to "irrelevant" depending on the run. The bold line below is the ground truth — none of the individual runs match it:
With small datasets (25–50 samples, typical for our CFD campaigns), a single SHAP iteration is essentially a point estimate with unknown variance. But downstream processes — DoE construction, design space reduction — treat these rankings as gospel. If the feature ranking is wrong, the entire surrogate model training pipeline is corrupted.
Slide 3 — What I've Tried
  • Bootstrap + SHAP: Train B=50 surrogate models on bootstrap resamples, compute SHAP for each, aggregate into box plots. This gives us variance estimates and reveals which features are reliably important vs. noise.
  • BIFI (Bootstrap-Informed Feature Importance): A new method that automatically identifies the threshold between "important" and "negligible" features using the cumulative SHAP distribution's maximum gradient — no manual cutoff needed.
  • Result: BIFI-based DoEs improve surrogate model quality (R² and MSE) by up to 90% compared to naive SHAP-based feature selection. But...
  • Open problem: The method works on synthetic benchmarks and one real dataset. I don't know how it generalizes across different model types, feature correlations, or when the "true" importance structure is highly non-linear.
Slide 4 — The Ask
  1. How do you handle the trust problem with XAI in practice? Do your engineers actually use SHAP plots, or do they ignore them?
  2. Has anyone compared SHAP-based feature selection to classical sensitivity analysis (Sobol indices, Morris screening) on the same engineering dataset? When does one outperform the other?
  3. Industry perspective: What level of "explainability" do you actually need to get a surrogate model approved for use in a design process? Is SHAP enough, or do you need causal arguments?
  4. Does anyone have experience with the EU AI Act (Article 86) requirements for explainability in engineering decision-making — and how that changes what we need from XAI tools?

"Balancing Breaks My Best Blades"

Clara Henkel, BTU Cottbus-Senftenberg · Henkel et al., GPPS Shanghai 2025

Slide 1 — The Context
I'm working on fan-blisk design optimization where two disciplines collide: aerodynamics wants the most efficient blade shape, and structural mechanics needs to balance the blisk by shifting blade sections axially and circumferentially to avoid stress hotspots. Traditionally these are solved sequentially — optimize aero first, then balance for stress — but the shifts from balancing change the aerodynamics.

The blade is parameterized across 21 radial sections with 8 parameters each (168 total, reduced to 16 design variables). CFD runs on Rolls-Royce's in-house HYDRA solver (~45 min per design on 40 cores), FE stress analysis via sc03 (~15 min). We need surrogates to make global optimization feasible.
Slide 2 — The Core Roadblock
No single surrogate model works for both objectives — and the stress constraint is the real headache.

For efficiency, Bayesian ridge regression dominates (R² = 0.98). But for maximum stress, it's the worst performer (R² = 0.58). SVR wins for stress (R² = 0.88) but is mediocre for efficiency. I'm forced to mix different model types in the same optimization loop.
TargetBest modelR²Worst modelR²
Efficiency ηBayesian ridge0.981kNN0.626
Stress σmaxSVR0.879Bayesian ridge0.576
Worse: the stress constraint can also be framed as a classification problem (feasible vs. infeasible), and classification surrogates give different answers than regression surrogates about which designs are safe. With only 300 CFD samples and a 16-dimensional design space, I can't tell which formulation to trust.
Slide 3 — What I've Tried
  • Tested 5 surrogate types (RF, kNN, SVR, MLP, Bayesian ridge) with full-factorial hyperparameter search — no single model is universally best, confirming the no-free-lunch theorem in practice.
  • Regression vs. classification for stress: Classification (RF, ROC AUC = 0.94) identifies feasible/infeasible regions well, but loses the magnitude information. Regression preserves magnitude but is less accurate near the critical stress threshold.
  • Reduced 2D problem (balancing shifts only) for validation — surrogates work beautifully here (R² > 0.96). But scaling to the full 16D problem degrades accuracy significantly.
  • Surrogate-based optimization with genetic algorithm — finds better designs than the baseline, but I can't guarantee the stress predictions are reliable enough for the optimizer not to propose structurally dangerous blades.
Slide 4 — The Ask
  1. How do you handle mixed-surrogate optimization where different objectives need fundamentally different model types? Is there a clean framework for this?
  2. Regression vs. classification for constraints: Has anyone found a principled way to decide which formulation to use — or a way to combine both?
  3. Industry perspective: When surrogate-based optimization proposes a design near the stress limit, what's your validation protocol before you trust it? Do you always re-run the full FE analysis?
  4. With 300 samples in 16 dimensions, are we fundamentally data-starved? Has anyone used transfer learning or multi-fidelity surrogates to bootstrap from cheaper simulations?
