About

Not your typical academic symposium

We are stepping away from polished, high-level presentations to create a space for deep technical discussions, networking, and practical problem-solving. This is a safe space to share what's actually hard about your research.

Problem Pitches

PhD students present their actual roadblocks — not their results. The audience becomes your brain-trust.

Impulse Talks

Short provocations from academia, industry, and startups to keep the energy high and the perspectives diverse.

Open Troubleshooting

Unstructured time for 1-on-1 whiteboarding, spontaneous collaborations, and deep-dive conversations.

The Format

The Anti-Pitch: 4 Slides, 10 Minutes

Each PhD student gets 30 minutes total: a 10-minute structured presentation followed by 20 minutes of dedicated group troubleshooting. Here's the strict 4-slide format:

  1. The Context (2 min)

    What is the overarching goal of your project? Keep it brief — just enough context for the room to understand your problem.

  2. The Core Roadblock (3 min)

    Zoom in on one or two specific technical problems you're facing right now. Buggy model? Dirty data? Methodological flaw? Hardware limitation?

  3. What I've Tried (3 min)

    What solutions have you attempted so far? Why did they fail or fall short?

  4. The Ask (2 min)

    Specific questions for the room. "Has anyone used X to solve Y?" or "How do you handle this type of noise in industry?"

The goal is not to prove you know everything — the goal is to leverage the brains in the room to help you break through your roadblock.

Example

"Can I Trust My Own Simulation Data?"

Here's what a real Anti-Pitch looks like — honest, specific, and technically grounded. Based on published work by Katja Müller, Brandenburg University of Applied Sciences.

Slide 1 — The Context
I'm investigating how fan blade geometry influences in-flight ice accretion on the NASA R67 low-pressure compressor rotor. The goal is to run a sensitivity study across 50 blade designs (DoE) to identify which geometric parameters drive ice buildup — and feed those insights into a multidisciplinary design optimization.

The simulation chain is fully automated: ParaBlade generates geometry, ANSYS CFX computes the flow field, and FENSAP-ICE handles droplet trajectories and ice growth.
Slide 2 — The Core Roadblock
My problem is trust in my own data.

To run 50 designs in a reasonable timeframe, I need a manageable grid resolution. But my grid independence study revealed alarming discrepancies:
Fine gridCoarse gridMedium grid
Ice mass1.64 g (ref)3.56 g (+117%)1.71 g (+4%)
Comp. time~6 h~10 min~4 h
The medium grid is acceptable (~4% deviation) but takes 4 hours per design — that's 200 hours for my 50-design DoE, and that's just the icing step. How wrong can my rankings be before the whole sensitivity study becomes meaningless?
Slide 3 — What I've Tried
  • Used the medium grid (1×106 nodes) as a compromise — but I don't know if the ~4% error is systematic or design-dependent.
  • Compared against published experimental data (Strazisar et al.) — matches at design point but diverges near stall.
  • Checked statistical indicators (Spearman correlations, CoI) — plausible but with 50 samples and non-linear interactions, I can't be sure the rankings are stable.
  • Considered running a subset on the fine grid for spot-checking — but there's no established method for deciding which designs to validate or how many are enough.
Slide 4 — The Ask
  1. How do you handle simulation fidelity vs. budget trade-offs in your DoE work? Is there a principled way to quantify the "ranking error" introduced by a coarser grid?
  2. Has anyone used adaptive sampling or surrogate models (Gaussian processes, etc.) to reduce the number of full-fidelity runs needed?
  3. Industry perspective: When you run large parameter studies, what's your threshold for "good enough" data quality?
  4. Does anyone have experience with multi-fidelity approaches — using coarse-grid results as a cheap predictor and fine-grid runs only where it matters?
